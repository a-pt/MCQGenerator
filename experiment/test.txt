Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) is a specialized type of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) designed to process sequential data, such as time-series forecasting, speech recognition, and language translation. Unlike traditional networks, LSTMs are engineered to remember vital information over long periods, making them incredibly effective at understanding context and predicting future steps based on past inputs.The secret to an LSTM's power lies in its unique internal structure, which replaces the repeating modules of standard RNNs with a cell state and a series of "gates". The cell state acts as a conveyor belt, carrying information straight through the entire chain of processing. The gates—specifically the forget gate, input gate, and output gate—are small neural networks that act as valves. They determine exactly which pieces of information are allowed onto the conveyor belt to be stored, which are updated, and which are discarded as noise.These gating mechanisms allow LSTMs to solve the "vanishing gradient" problem that plagues traditional networks during training. By carefully regulating the flow of memory, LSTMs can maintain a continuous memory flow across long time intervals. Today, they remain a foundational architecture for natural language processing and sequential modeling, providing critical functionality for tools like predictive text, stock analysis, and voice assistants.
